Acids, Bases and Salts — Class Notes

Generated from tonight's live class with Vihaan.

1. What is an acid?

An acid is any substance that releases hydrogen ions (H⁺) when dissolved in water. Acids taste sour and turn blue litmus paper red. Examples: hydrochloric acid (HCl) in your stomach, citric acid in lemon, acetic acid in vinegar.

Key idea

Stronger acid = more H⁺ ions released = lower pH.

Remember

Acids in concentrated form are corrosive — always add acid to water, never the other way around.

2. What is a base?

A base releases hydroxide ions (OH⁻) when dissolved in water. Bases taste bitter and feel soapy. They turn red litmus paper blue. Examples: sodium hydroxide (NaOH) in soaps, calcium hydroxide (Ca(OH)₂) — slaked lime, magnesium hydroxide in antacids.

3. The pH scale

pH measures how acidic or basic a solution is, on a scale from 0 to 14.

4. Reactions of acids

  • AWith metals: Acid + Metal → Salt + Hydrogen gas. Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂↑
  • BWith carbonates: Acid + Carbonate → Salt + CO₂ + Water. (This is why baking soda fizzes with vinegar.)
  • CWith bases (neutralisation): Acid + Base → Salt + Water. HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O

5. Important salts

  • Common salt — NaCl: table salt; also the source of all other "sodium-something" chemicals.
  • Baking soda — NaHCO₃: in idli batter, in antacids; reacts with acid to release CO₂.
  • Washing soda — Na₂CO₃: in detergents, glass manufacture.
  • Plaster of Paris — CaSO₄·½H₂O: hardens by absorbing water; used for casts, decorations.
